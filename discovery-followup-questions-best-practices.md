# Best Practices: Follow-Up Questions in Product Discovery

> Based on Teresa Torres (Continuous Discovery Habits), Nielsen Norman Group, and Product Talk research.

---

## 1. Work Backwards from Solution to Problem (Torres)

When a stakeholder submits a feature request, they've already solution-framed it. Your job is to excavate the problem underneath.

> *"Which outcome or opportunity are you trying to address with this?"*
> *"If we had that solution — how would we measure it's working?"*

---

## 2. Anchor to a Specific Real Instance — Not Hypotheticals

Never ask what people want in general. Ask about the last specific time.

> *"Tell me about the last time you experienced this problem."*
> *"What happened first? Then what?"*

Hypotheticals reveal opinions. Specific instances reveal behavior. Follow-up questions should force chronological, factual answers — not preferences.

---

## 3. Separate Pain from Frequency

Two dimensions determine how much a problem actually matters:

- **How painful is it?** — What happens when it fails? What's the workaround cost?
- **How often does it happen?** — Is this daily friction or a once-a-quarter edge case?

Both must be asked. High pain + rare = overstated urgency. Low pain + daily = underrated problem.

---

## 4. Expose the Workaround

The workaround is the most honest signal you have:

> *"What do you do today when this happens?"*

- Fast and painless workaround → problem is not urgent
- Manual, slow, or embarrassing workaround → real, unmet need

---

## 5. Validate Scope Before Validating Solution

Before discussing solutions, validate who else has this problem:

> *"Do you know if others on your team face this too?"*
> *"Is this something you've heard from customers directly?"*

Internal stakeholders often speak for themselves while implying they speak for many.

---

## 6. Link to Business Outcome Explicitly (Torres via Product Talk)

> *"If we had that solution, what would that do for our business?"*

Forces the stakeholder to articulate impact — and often reveals they haven't thought past the request itself.

---

## 7. Ask What Would Change the Priority

> *"What would need to be true for this to be urgent?"*

Surfaces hidden dependencies, timelines, and conditions. Often reveals the request is contingent on something else happening first.

---

## 8. Never Ask Leading or Loaded Questions (NN/g Principle)

- Never combine two questions into one
- Never suggest the answer
- Keep questions neutral and singular

| Bad | Good |
|-----|------|
| "Don't you think this would help users a lot?" | "How do you think this would affect users?" |
| "Wouldn't this save a lot of time?" | "What would change about your workflow if this existed?" |

---

## Summary Framework

| Category | Best Question |
|---|---|
| Excavate the problem | "Tell me about the last time this happened — walk me through it." |
| Measure success | "How would we know if we solved it?" |
| Business impact | "What would this do for the business if we built it?" |
| Workaround | "What do you do today when this occurs?" |
| Scope | "Is this something others are experiencing too?" |
| Frequency | "How often does this come up?" |
| Pain | "What's the cost when it happens?" |
| Priority trigger | "What would need to be true for this to be urgent?" |
